The Beginning

Before all the events that led to this book took place Daniel and I were both studying at university: I was finishing my last year, Daniel was at the end of his third; we were a pretty normal couple living in a small city of the Italian coast, before chaos changed our lives, a chaos that started many centuries, even millennia, ago. In the past couple of years, little by little most of the countries in the world had started wars with each other, and the relatively small wars had merged in a bigger conflict when alliances had started to grow. The result was what some people were already calling World War III, a complex conflict with many sides, of which the most prominent were the North American Union, the Coalition of Eastern Asia and the North African Common Republic. The only reason it hadn't escalated into nuclear warfare, yet, was that no one of the groups knew what was the nuclear power of the others. At the same time, the planet was shaken by natural disasters nearly every month; because of the war, geologists all over the world were unable to share their discoveries, if they ever happened to make any, so the only certain thing was that the frequency of earthquakes had skyrocketed, and that volcanoes were appearing in places where they shouldn't. As had happened in other periods of crisis throughout history, people started to believe that the end of the world was near. Religions gained followers by the thousands.

And still, Italy was calm. In a shocking turn of events the European Union, that up to mere months before the explosion of chaos was crumbling to pieces under the weight of corruption, xenophobia and nationalism, had been renewed by a wave of youth. A new generation of politicians had gained votes appealing to the inaction of their predecessors, and then managed to fix the single nations, and consequently the Union itself. Thanks to this renewal, when the war started getting menacingly close the states were able to organize together a line of defense, granting us, as citizens, a somewhat stable peace. This new idea of unity saved us and the other nations again when Middle East raised the prices to unreachable levels and our oil-based society nearly fell: the research of alternative energy was financed more than ever. By consequence, all the nations in the Union benefitted from the shared new low-cost energy production. Still, the tension of war was there, and the nations that made up the border of Europe, like Italy, were painfully aware that the line of defense could not hold forever.

In this precarious equilibrium, I was months away from getting my Master's degree in Theoretical Physics, and my professor had already offered me the opportunity to join a multidisciplinary international project in Germany that he was involved with. I was thrilled by the opportunity, I would meet students from all over Europe and all fields of science, while doing research, which was my dream; still, this twist of fate came unannounced, while I was deciding what to actually do with my life. To go would mean staying away from my city for at least three years and maybe more, basically leaving my family and my friends behind, and not seeing Daniel for months at a time, I was scared that our relationship would fall to pieces, with the distance and all. My family obviously encouraged me to go, to pursuit my dream.

"Jake, this is the occasion you've been waiting for since you started the university. You'll meet new people, make new friends, and over all you'll grow up as a person. In this historical moment, with all the stuff going on in the world, these occasions are very rare, don't waste the one you've been offered," said my mum. My friends were very supportive too, but the reaction I feared the most was Daniel's. When I told him I was even more nervous than the first time I asked him out, with sweating palms and shaking voice.

"Sweetie, I need to tell you something." We were at his place, sitting on the couch, both covered with a plaid, drinking hot chocolate.

*

I remember that evening! You were being so dramatic… I understood that something wasn't right when you put down the cup with some chocolate still inside. I've only seen you do that in a few occasions, and every time it was something big. Well, you were right, I was telling you that in the next years we would see each other at most once a month, I think that's something big, don't you? Like I told you that time, we can… Yes, I remember what you said, give me a minute and I get there. Now, where were we?

*

I put down my cup, and grabbed his hands. "My professor offered to save me a spot in ITOWN after I get my degree. If I decide to go I'll stay away for at least two years, maybe more, and I'll come home once a month, or less," I said in one breath. "I don't know if I actually want to go."

"Why? It's been your dream since you were a kid, to do the real science, and now you don't want to?" Daniel was amazing as always.

"It isn't that I don't want to go, but I don't want to leave you behind. I don't want to wake up one morning and discover that I don't love you anymore. I don't want to follow my dream if this comes with abandoning you. I just…"

"Hey, calm down. You're not leaving me behind. We're both young, and if one morning you realize that you don't love me, maybe we were not meant for each other; but I think we are. We can be on opposite sides of the planet, and we would still be a power couple."

"So you think I should go?"

"Definitely."

I leant to kiss him, then I took my cup back.

"You know I'll call you every day, right?"

"I know, sweetie," Daniel chuckled.

After that conversation, my mind was finally settled; I started the preparations while still working on my thesis: I was finishing the analysis of data coming from the LHC, the Large Hadron Collider, and the results I was getting had something strange in them. While they were in line with theory, there seemed to be a sort of weird interference I couldn't explain. My professor agreed with me, and reported it to the LHC maintenance, saying that probably there was a problem with the isolation of the tunnel. When they checked that a problem was actually there, but couldn't find a cause, they set on doing some more research; I went on with my thesis, and finished it in time to get my degree in physics before the end of June. Less than a month after that, I was ready to take the train that would take me to an EU facility dedicated to scientific research of every field, in northern Germany. Scientists from all over Europe were called there when the energy crisis started, and in no time the International Ecology Centre became the International Centre for Natural Studies, and then the Institution for Technologic Observation and Withstanding of Nature, ITOWN for short. Even after my professor described it very thoroughly, the first impression was a shock: it looked like a small town, developed around a central white building, the original IEC, with actual houses in which lived the researchers and the people working in the many buildings. I was going to share one of those houses with other three students who graduated in the last year, all from different fields, in a philosophy of "cultural exchange" between the different subjects that were studied there. The professor only took me to the facility, then he would go back to his job at the university in a few days; when we arrived, in late afternoon, he left me at the door of what would become my home for the next years with my key and my luggage, and left to set an experiment on track with his research group.

I opened the door, scared and excited at once, and entered a white hallway, at the end of which there was a big library, going up to the ceiling. I closed the door behind me, put down my bags and took a few steps down the hall, when I saw a short girl come out of a room near the library.

"Hey, you're the new guy! They said we would have a new housemate, I'm glad we had an empty room," she said smiling, with a faint French accent, "I'm Marie Beauchene, biologist." She came closer to me and held her hand up.

"I'm Jake Berti, physicist," I shook her hand, smiling back at her, "Where can I put my things?"

"Oh, right! That's your room," she pointed to one of the four doors lined at my right, "they're not enormous, but there's definitely enough space." After I moved my things from the hallway, I asked her if the others were outside.

"No, they're closed in their rooms, as always." She shouted towards two closed doors near mine, "Tore! Leonore! Come greet the new guy!" After a few seconds, one of the doors opened just enough to let a mass of blond curly hair appear, followed by a pair of ice blue eyes and the rest of a quite tall guy.

"Hey, welcome! I'm Tore Beck, computer scientist." He had a thick northern accent, maybe from Norway or Sweden. "I'm sorry I didn't hear you, I was working on a project."

"No you weren't, you were playing online as usual." A Spanish or Portuguese girl with long straight black hair peeked from the other room "I'm Leonore Arce by the way, mechanical engineering. I'm glad I was still here, I was getting ready to go to work, I nearly missed you. A few rules: never enter my room, and if you absolutely must at least don't touch anything on the desk. You can borrow my books, but the first time I find any of them ruined the culprit pays for it. My computers are off limits in every circumstance, except if I say so. Everything clear?" I answered with a firm "Yes ma'am!"

We immediately exchanged telephone numbers, and they added me in their group chat, then Tore went back in his room, and while Leonore was getting ready Marie showed me around; the house wasn't very big, but it had everything: a living room with a small kitchen area, a shared bathroom and a closet. Then she brought me upstairs and showed me the best part: a whole floor dedicated to a multidisciplinary laboratory, filled with all the equipment needed for most experiments.

"You're probably going to spend a lot of time here. We sure do; at the beginning it was just the excitement of having a lab at home, then it became more of a second living room. Obviously the usual rules about labs apply, like no food and all that, but for the rest you can do what you want here. Leonore even installed a stereo, so you can come here, listen to some music, read a book, anything. The place is completely soundproof, because of some particularly loud machines, so if you ever want to listen to heavy metal in the middle of the night you just come here, close the door, and play whatever you want."

"This is fantastic," I replied in awe. "And we can do what we want with it?"

"Yes. Well, its official purpose is to allow us to bring some work home, for example sometimes I happen to have still analysis to do at the end of my shift, so I bring the samples home and finish here, but at the end of the day until you break something no one is going to tell you anything for doing what you want at your home."

While we went downstairs she asked, "So, what do you think of your new house?"

"It's amazing, and you guys are brilliant!"

"Thank you," she smiled.

Lenore had already left when we got in the kitchen, where Tore was cooking some fish; while we ate they explained to me how they decided to manage food; each of us had half a cupboard, plus a whole shared one, and a shelf in the fridge. Since the house had been built less than a year before, the kitchen was new, but Lenore had decided that the oven was off-limits for the whole summer, so she did her magic and now it was impossible to turn on.

"You'll discover that she does that a lot," explained Thora. "If she decides that we need something she builds it in a minute, but if she decides that something is annoying she turns it off in even less time. She's very impulsive, but the bright side is that last February she woke up freezing in the middle of the night and before noon the heating system was upgraded in the whole house."

"In time you'll learn to interpret her; it's not difficult after all, she's very straight-forward: most times she simply finds an issue and solves it. The only problem is when you don't agree with her on said issue," added Marie.

In the evening Daniel texted me asking if I wanted to video chat; I was dead tired by the long train ride, and I was still unpacking my things, so we just texted, but I promised we would see each other the next day.

After a night of nervous sleep, I went to the physics building to complete my registration. My research group would be studying the causes of an interference similar to the one that I had found writing my thesis; what at the time looked like a strange but quite insignificant maintenance problem, had gained a lot of weight in the next few weeks, when a similar data distortion showed up in nearly all the particle physics and cosmology related experiments performed. We would be trying to understand a cause, along with other groups from the few other scientific facilities around the world that had managed not to be fully dedicated to war research. As I entered the building, I found myself in a huge hall sunlit through a glass roof, full of people just arriving like me or going home after a night of work; just at the entrance there was the receptionist's desk, where a well-dressed man was typing on a keyboard

"Hello," I approached the desk. The man smiled:

"Hi, I'm Aleksandr, how can I help you?"

"I'm Jake Berti, I arrived yesterday from Italy, I need to complete my registration." He typed something on the computer:

"Oh, yes, Jake, you'll just need to sign some papers, then they'll give you your badge and some brochures about ITOWN, just follow the corridor," he pointed at his left. "The registration office is the last one on the right. If you ever need something, just ask me." After I thanked him, I followed his directions and got to the office. There a secretary explained me what I was allowed to do and what I wasn't, how lunch and dinner times worked, what the badge was for and how generally things worked there at the physics department, all this while she made me sign a couple security and privacy papers. When we were finished I asked her when I would meet the others.

"Well, because the last of your teammates is arriving in a couple days we can't officially form the group, yet, so I can't give you their telephone numbers; since Candice will be in charge of your group, she already has your contact information, when everything's ready she will send you an email," she concluded with a smile.

I spent the next few days waiting for the email, video calling Daniel, and getting used to my new place; Marie and Tore were on a break with their groups, so they decided to become my mentors, showing me around the city and giving me advices about the best cafeterias and bars, the most convenient places for grocery shopping, and generally taking me city-sighting. Since Lenore was working I barely saw her, until the third day I was there she finally had a free evening and proposed to cook dinner for everyone, so we got to spend some time together.

Two more days later I finally received the email, and the next morning I went to meet the others at the physics department; Aleksandr eyed me as soon as I stepped inside.

"Jake! Hi! They sent you the email?"

"Hi Aleksandr! Yes, I have to meet my group in the PP4 laboratory."

"PP4, alright! It's in the second basement floor, go down that corridor, – he pointed across the hallway – you'll find both the elevators and the stairs. Once you're down there just follow the signs, they're all over the place."

I thanked him, and following the instructions I got to the lab, a small room, just enough space for the five desks and the informatic equipment we were going to use. Three people were already inside, sitting in front of the computers.

"Good morning, I'm Jake Berti," I greeted the others. A tall woman somewhat older than me spoke first:

"Good morning! I'm Candice Villeneuve, I'll be in charge of the group. These are Yun Zhu and Faraji Mukantagara, - she gestured towards the others, a slender Chinese girl and a very muscular Nigerian guy both about my age, and I shook their hands - and Georg should join us soon. As I was telling them, you're all at your first real lab experience, so don't worry if you need some time to settle in, but at the same time we're here to work. We're going to look into a new form of radiation that has been discovered lately..." she was interrupted by a guy with very dark hair knocking on the door.

"Hello, I'm Georg Brandt." He spoke with a quite thick German accent.

"Oh, hi, Georg," Candice greeted. She introduced herself again, and then introduced Yun, Faraji and me, like she did before.

"Now that you're all here, we can officially start: as I was saying, we're going to do some research about a strange radiation kind that was discovered in the last few months. It influenced a great deal of experiments all over the world, or at least in the parts that still try to do some research, and the only reliable conclusion is that it's something we never met in the past." While she was speaking, I could see that the others were as excited as I was; she continued, "We'll be trying to understand some properties of this radiation, and whether or not it's dangerous. To do this, ITOWN granted us almost free access to the particle accelerator and to the orbital telescope, and by that I mean that while the waiting list for both is incredibly long, we can use them any time they're not booked, without the need for an evaluation by the committees. Living here, you'll find out that the bureaucracy is hell, so skipping the evaluation is a breath of fresh air. Yes?" Yun had raised her hand.

"I think I missed I part. Why do we need both the accelerator and the telescope? Aren't we going to study particle physics?"

"Well, that's the point: the reason this radiation, and our research by association, is so important is that it appears in both particle physics and cosmology. Some believe it could be a link between General Relativity and the Standard Model, others think it could be the finishing touch to String Theory... The only thing everyone agrees on is that it's weird, and it needs studying. This is also the reason why you and Jake are specialized in particle physics, while Georg and Faraji are specialized in cosmology.
